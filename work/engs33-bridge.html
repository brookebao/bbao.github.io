Making a Truss Bridge

ENGS 33: Solid Mechanics @ Dartmouth College
Structural Design · SAP2000 · SolidWorks Simulation + FEA· Load Testing
Overview Ideation Design & Analysis Testing Results Deliverables
Project Snapshot
  • Goals: Design a scale-model truss bridge under geometric and material constraints; maximize strength and stiffness while keeping bridge mass low; compare predicted vs actual deflection and failure behavior.
  • Software: SAP2000, SolidWorks, SolidWorks Simulation.
  • Skills: FEA interpretation, iterative design, fabrication and assembly, laser cutting.

Final bridge drawing / render.

Ideation

The design process began with research into existing truss bridge types, followed by multiple preliminary sketches exploring geometry, member layout, and overall form.

Design direction: Rather than directly copying a standard Warren, Howe, or Pratt bridge, the final concept combined ideas from precedent research, group sketches, and practical build constraints.
Design & Analysis

After selecting a concept, the bridge was iteratively refined through analytical and simulation-based modeling. SAP2000 was used to compare variations in geometry and member layout, while SolidWorks Simulation was used to evaluate stress, displacement, and likely failure regions.

Key design variables

Height, member orientation, bracing strategy, and member thickness.

What was optimized

Lower stress concentration, lower deflection, and better performance per unit mass.

Decision point

The final configuration was selected based on predicted stiffness, strength, and constructability.

Testing
Test setup

The bridge was tested under a centered loading configuration using the Instron, with failure load and midspan deflection compared against predictions.

What we tracked
  • Deflection under load
  • Maximum load before failure
  • Failure location and failure mode
  • Predicted vs actual behavior
Results

Final Outcome

The final bridge successfully translated analytical design choices into measurable structural performance, while also revealing where real-world fabrication and connection effects differed from idealized models.
